Bioinformatics Day — Honoring Margaret Dayhoff

🧬 Today, March 11th, is Bioinformatics Day — and it's no coincidence. This date was chosen to honor Margaret Dayhoff, a trailblazer who changed science forever.

In the 1940s and 50s, when computers were the size of rooms and biology was purely "wet lab" work, Dayhoff had a radical idea: use computation to understand life itself. She created the first protein sequence databases, developed one-letter amino acid codes still used today, and pioneered the concept of evolutionary phylogenetics through sequence comparison. All before most people knew what a computer was.

Her work laid the foundation for everything we now call bioinformatics — from genome assembly and variant calling to single-cell RNA-seq, trajectory analysis, and AI-driven drug discovery.

Fast forward to today: → The human genome has been fully sequenced → AlphaFold predicted the structure of nearly every known protein → Single-cell technologies let us profile gene expression one cell at a time → Bioinformaticians work at the intersection of biology, statistics, and software engineering to drive discoveries that save lives

All of it traces back to people like Dayhoff — scientists who dared to ask: what if we treated biology as data?

To every bioinformatician, computational biologist, and data scientist in the life sciences today: this day is yours. 🙌
