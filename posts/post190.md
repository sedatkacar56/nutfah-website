🧬 Everyone knows KEGG. We all use it. We run pathview. We cite the diagrams. We love the visuals. But here's something most people don't talk about: KEGG covers around 350 human pathways. That's it. And if your pathway of interest isn't there? You hit a wall. I've been there too.

💡 Did you know WikiPathways exists?

WikiPathways was established in 2008 — founded by a community of scientists who wanted something KEGG wasn't: Open. Free. Expandable. Think of it as the Wikipedia of biological pathways. Anyone can contribute. Anyone can correct. Anyone can add.

🔬 Did you know...
• WikiPathways covers 800+ human pathways — more than double KEGG
• It is 100% free and open — no API restrictions, no licensing headaches
• It is community-driven — updated by researchers around the world
• It includes pathways from recent publications KEGG hasn't added yet
• Disease-specific pathways? WikiPathways has them.
• It has its own R package: rWikiPathways — integrates with clusterProfiler and Bioconductor

📊 Quick comparison:
KEGG | WikiPathways
Human pathways ~350 | 800+
Free API Partial | Full
Community editable No | Yes
R package pathview | rWikiPathways
Updated by Central team | Global scientists

✅ Why WikiPathways stands out:
1️⃣ More coverage — especially disease-specific and signaling pathways KEGG misses
2️⃣ Fully open — no restrictions, reproducible research friendly
3️⃣ Constantly growing — built by the same researchers who need it
4️⃣ Machine readable format (GPML) — similar to KEGG's KGML
5️⃣ Visualizations — pathway diagrams just as clean and publication-ready

🧠 So next time your pathway isn't in KEGG... Don't stop there. Check WikiPathways first. You might find exactly what you need — built by someone who had the same problem as you. Both databases have their strengths. The smart move is knowing when to use which.
