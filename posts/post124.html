Doublets vs Transitional States in Single-Cell RNA-seq

🔬 One of the Biggest Challenges in Single-Cell RNA-seq: Distinguishing Doublets, Mixed-Cell Transcriptomes, and True Transitional States

In single-cell analysis, one of the hardest tasks is separating technical artifacts from biological reality. As datasets grow — thousands of cells per sample, multimodal experiments, and more diverse cell atlases — we constantly face a central question:

👉 Is this cluster a genuine transitional cell population, or just a mixture of mRNA coming from doublets or experimental noise?

This distinction isn't always obvious. Sometimes two cell types are physically close, captured together, and their transcriptomes blend. Other times, a cell is truly in transition — shifting identity, rewiring gene networks, and showing hybrid molecular signatures.

And here is where we must "add just the right amount of salt" — meaning:

⚖️ apply careful judgment
⚖️ validate with multiple tools
⚖️ avoid over-interpreting ambiguous clusters

Because misreading transitional signals as doublets (or vice-versa) can dramatically change downstream conclusions.

As single-cell datasets scale up, this challenge is becoming even more important. The future of scRNA-seq depends not only on generating more cells, but on interpreting them with precision, biological context, and caution.
