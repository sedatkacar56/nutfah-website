CD14 in Single-Cell RNA-Seq — Understanding the Biology Behind the Marker

🧬 Don't just memorize markers. Understand why they're expressed where they are. That understanding changes how you annotate cells and how you interpret ambiguous results.

CD14 as an example:

CD14 functions as a co-receptor — a "spotter, not the lifter." It captures lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from gram-negative bacteria and delivers it to the TLR4/MD-2 complex, which triggers an immune response cascade involving NF-κB activation and inflammatory cytokine release.

Why is CD14 high on monocytes? Monocytes serve as first responders to bacterial infection. They patrol the blood and require heightened sensitivity to LPS. High CD14 expression gives them maximum sensitivity to detect bacterial threats as early as possible.

Critical context: while macrophages also express CD14, the marker's meaning depends on context. In peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), high CD14 specifically indicates monocytes — because macrophages are tissue-resident cells not found in peripheral blood samples.

🧠 Understanding receptor functions, ligand interactions, and signaling pathways transforms cell annotation from computational labeling into genuine biological discovery. Ask not just "what is this marker?" but "why is this gene here in this cell?"
